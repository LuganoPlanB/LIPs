# What is a LIP?

LIP stands for Lugano Improvement Proposal. A LIP is a structured design document addressed to the city of Lugano and its ecosystem of stakeholders. It proposes an improvement to civic processes, public services, or the city’s digital infrastructure.

A LIP should provide a concise specification of the proposed change, its rationale, and the anticipated impact. The goal is to make proposals understandable, reviewable, and actionable across technical and non-technical actors.

LIPs are intended to be the primary mechanism for:

* proposing improvements and new initiatives with civic purpose
* collecting structured public and stakeholder input on an issue
* documenting the reasoning and decisions behind adopted changes
* keeping a public record of proposals, outcomes, and iterations

The LIP author is responsible for describing the proposal clearly, engaging relevant stakeholders, and documenting significant objections or alternative viewpoints.

Because LIPs are maintained as text files in a versioned repository, their revision history is part of the public record of how a proposal evolved.

## LIP Types

There are three kinds of LIP:

* A Standards Track LIP describes a change that affects interoperability, shared city infrastructure, or cross-department coordination. Examples include common data formats, identity and access principles, procurement interoperability requirements, public API guidelines, service integration conventions, or shared operational standards.

* An Informational LIP describes an issue, a design space, or background research, or provides general guidelines and best practices relevant to civic digital infrastructure. Informational LIPs do not necessarily represent consensus or a recommendation, and may be used as reference material.

* A Process LIP describes or proposes changes to how decisions are made, how proposals are evaluated, how public consultation is conducted, or how implementation and accountability are tracked. Process LIPs govern how the LIP system itself works, and related civic governance workflows.

## LIP Work Flow

The LIP process begins with a new idea or identified problem affecting the city’s digital infrastructure or civic processes.

Each potential LIP should have a champion, someone who writes the LIP using the style and format described below, shepherds discussion in the appropriate forums, and attempts to build consensus among stakeholders. Stakeholders may include city departments, service operators, local businesses, civil society groups, technical communities, and residents.

Vetting an idea publicly before writing a full LIP is encouraged. It saves time, reduces duplication, and helps identify constraints early. A proposal may be locally useful yet unsuitable as a city-wide standard, or may require legal, operational, or budget alignment that should be surfaced early.

Once the champion believes the idea is mature enough, they submit a draft LIP to the designated discussion channel and repository. The draft should be written in LIP style, otherwise it can be returned for revision.

Authors are responsible for collecting feedback on both the initial idea and the draft LIP before submitting it for editorial review. Long open-ended discussions should be avoided where possible. Strategies to keep review efficient include time-boxed consultations, issue trackers with clear questions, stakeholder workshops with minutes, and structured requests for comment.

It is recommended that a single LIP contain a single key proposal. If the scope becomes too broad, split the work into multiple LIPs.

LIP editors assign LIP numbers and manage status changes. Authors must not self-assign LIP numbers and should instead use an alias during drafting, for example:

```
lip-jaromil-digital-identity
lip-jaromil-lost&found-revocation
```

If a LIP is accepted for consideration, editors assign a number, set the Type, give it status "Draft", and add it to the repository.

Reasons for denying LIP status include duplication of effort, lack of clarity, disregard for formatting rules, scope that is too broad, technically or operationally unsound proposals, missing rationale, missing stakeholder considerations, or proposals that do not serve a civic purpose.

The author may update the Draft in the repository. Updates may be submitted as changesets or pull requests, depending on the hosting workflow.

Standards Track LIPs generally include two parts: the design document and an implementation plan. They should be reviewed and agreed in principle before major implementation begins, unless a pilot is necessary to evaluate feasibility.

A Standards Track LIP should include an implementation approach before it can be considered Final. "Implementation" here does not necessarily mean software code. It can include a procurement plan, operational runbooks, governance changes, rollout stages, and measurable acceptance criteria.

Once a LIP has been adopted and its implementation has completed (or reached an agreed stable state), its status is changed to "Final".

A LIP can also be assigned status "Deferred" when no progress is being made, or when prerequisites are missing. It may later be reactivated.

A LIP can be "Rejected". It remains important to keep a record, including the reasons.

A LIP can also be superseded by a different LIP, rendering the original obsolete. This is common when a proposal is replaced by a revised approach.

Some Informational and Process LIPs may have a status of "Active" if they are not meant to be completed, but to remain as living guidance or standing procedure.

## What belongs in a successful LIP?

Each LIP should have the following parts:

* Preamble
  RFC 822 style headers containing metadata about the LIP, including the LIP number, a short title, authors, status, type, and dates.

* Abstract
  A short description of the issue being addressed and the proposed improvement.

* Motivation
  Why the change is needed in civic terms. What problem exists today, for whom, and what evidence supports the claim. This section is critical and may include user impact, operational friction, costs, risks, and strategic alignment.

* Specification
  A clear description of what will change. For Standards Track LIPs this should include any interfaces, data formats, governance rules, or operational requirements needed for interoperability or coordination. It should be detailed enough that different implementers can produce compatible outcomes where compatibility matters.

* Rationale
  Why this design was chosen. What alternatives were considered. What trade-offs are being made. Reference prior art where relevant, including approaches used in other cities or public administrations.

* Stakeholder and Impact Analysis
  Who is affected, how, and what the expected benefits and risks are. Include accessibility, inclusion, privacy, security, sustainability, maintenance burden, vendor lock-in risk, and budget implications where applicable.

* Consultation and Dissent
  Evidence of consultation and the main objections raised, including how they were addressed or why they were not adopted.

* Compatibility and Transition
  If the proposal changes existing services, contracts, data flows, or user-facing procedures, describe the transition path, timelines, and mitigation of disruptions. The goal is continuity of service and clear accountability.

* Implementation Plan
  Steps, responsibilities, prerequisites, dependencies, and acceptance criteria. This may include pilots, procurement, governance approvals, operational readiness, documentation, and monitoring.

* Governance and Accountability
  How decisions are made during rollout, who owns the outcome, how progress is reported, and what triggers revision or rollback.

* Licensing and Reuse
  State whether the text is public domain or under a permissive documentation license suitable for reuse by other municipalities.

### LIP Formats and Templates

LIPs should be written in plain text using mediawiki or markdown format, with a preference for formats that are easy to review in version control.

### LIP Header Preamble

Each LIP must begin with an RFC 822 style header preamble. The headers must appear in the following order. Headers marked with "*" are optional. All other headers are required.

```
  LIP: <LIP number>
  Title: <LIP title>
  Author: <list of authors' real names and optionally, email addrs>
* Discussions-To: <url or email address>
  Status: <Draft | Active | Accepted | Deferred | Rejected |
           Withdrawn | Final | Superseded>
  Type: <Standards Track | Informational | Process>
  Created: <date created on, in ISO 8601 (yyyy-mm-dd) format>
* Post-History: <dates of public postings or consultation rounds>
* Replaces: <LIP number>
* Superseded-By: <LIP number>
* Resolution: <url to decision record, council resolution, or published outcome>
```

The Author header lists the names, and optionally the email addresses of all the authors/owners of the LIP. Each author should be on a separate line if there are multiple authors.

The Discussions-To header should indicate where the LIP is being discussed: a public consultation page, an issue tracker, a mailing list, or an official city channel. It can be omitted if discussion is being handled privately in early drafting, but public discussion is expected before acceptance where feasible.

The Created header records the date the LIP was assigned a number. Post-History records dates of major publication steps, consultation rounds, workshops, or public review milestones.

The Resolution header is recommended for Standards Track LIPs and Process LIPs. It should point to an official record of adoption, decision, or outcome.

### Auxiliary Files

LIPs may include auxiliary files such as diagrams. Auxiliary files should be included in a subdirectory for that LIP, or must be named LIP-XXXX-Y.ext, where "XXXX" is the LIP number, "Y" is a serial number (starting at 1), and "ext" is replaced by the actual file extension (e.g. "png").


## LIP Editors

The LIP editors are the maintainers responsible for administrative and editorial oversight of the LIP repository and process. They can be contacted via the repository’s designated contact point.

### Transferring LIP Ownership

It may become necessary to transfer ownership of a LIP to a new champion. A good reason is that the original author no longer has time or interest in maintaining the proposal through review and implementation. A poor reason is disagreement with the proposal’s direction, in which case competing proposals should be documented as separate LIPs.

A request to assume ownership should be made to the original author and the LIP editors. If the original author is unreachable, the editors may decide to reassign stewardship to keep the process moving, while preserving authorship history.

### LIP Editor Responsibilities & Workflow

For each new LIP submission, editors:

* Review the LIP for readiness: clarity, completeness, civic relevance, and fit to scope.
* Check that the title accurately describes the content.
* Suggest editorial improvements to language and structure.
* Ensure the proposal includes sufficient rationale, stakeholder impact considerations, and a transition plan where relevant.

If the LIP is not ready, editors return it to the author with specific revision requests.

Once ready, the LIP is submitted to the repository through the standard contribution workflow (for example, a pull request), where it may receive further public feedback.

Editors typically:

* Assign a LIP number.
* Merge the submission when it meets baseline criteria.
* Ensure it is listed in the repository index.
* Point the author to the next step in the review and consultation process.
